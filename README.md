## Problem Statement
EcoSnap addresses the widespread confusion and environmental damage caused by improper waste disposal by providing users with an AI-powered tool that instantly identifies scanned waste items, reveals their environmental impact, and delivers actionable, item-specific recycling and disposal instructions.

## Project Description
EcoSnap – Brief Project Description

What's our solution? EcoSnap is an AI-powered web app that acts as a personal recycling assistant, helping users easily identify waste and dispose of it correctly.
How it works? Users simply snap or upload a photo of their trash. The AI instantly scans the image, identifies the material (e.g., plastic, e-waste, organic), explains its environmental impact, and provides exact, step-by-step disposal instructions.
What makes it useful? It eliminates the confusion around complex recycling rules, preventing both "wish-cycling" (contaminating recycling bins) and throwing good recyclables into landfills, ultimately educating users and fostering long-term sustainable habits.


## Google AI Usage
How Google AI is Used in EcoSnap (Brief)

EcoSnap relies on Google AI (like Gemini Vision or Google Cloud Vision) to power its core functionality:

Vision AI for Identification: When a user uploads a photo of waste, the AI instantly scans and accurately identifies the specific material (e.g., PET plastic, cardboard, e-waste).
Generative AI for Guidance: Based on the identified object, the AI generates real-time, context-aware information, instantly calculating its environmental impact (like decomposition time) and outputting precise, step-by-step disposal instructions.
In short, Google AI acts as the "brain," turning an everyday image into actionable, sustainable advice.


- 

### How Google AI Was Used
Think of Google AI as having two main parts in this app: The Eyes and The Brain.

1. The Eyes (Image Recognition)
When a user takes a picture of a piece of trash (like a crumpled coffee cup or an old battery), EcoSnap sends that image to Google Gemini Vision (Google's advanced image-reading AI).

What it does: The AI "looks" at the picture and figures out exactly what the object is and what material it is made of.
Example: Instead of just seeing a "cup," the AI recognizes it as a "paper coffee cup with a plastic inner lining."
2. The Brain (Information Processing)
Once the eyes have identified the object, the app uses Google's Generative AI (the text-generating side of Gemini) to act as the brain.

What it does: It takes the identified item (the coffee cup) and instantly searches its vast knowledge base to answer key questions:
Is this recyclable, compostable, or trash?
How long does this take to break down in nature?
What are the exact steps to throw it away safely?
3. The Final Output (What the User Sees)
Google AI instantly packages all of this information and displays it clearly on the user's screen in milliseconds.

The Result: The user sees:
Item Identified: Paper Coffee Cup.
Environmental Impact: "Takes roughly 20-30 years to decompose because of the plastic lining."
Actionable Advice: "Remove the plastic lid and recycle it. Throw the main paper cup in the regular trash, as the plastic lining makes it unrecyclable in most facilities."
